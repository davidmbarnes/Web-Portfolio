A Tribute to Carl Sagan

"Somewhere, something incredible is waiting to be known."

- Carl Sagan

Early Years

Born on November 9, 1934, in Brooklyn, New York, Carl Edward Sagan, From nuclear disarmament, to extraterrestrial intelligence, to 'Cosmos: A Personal Voyage,' Carl Sagan was one of the most influential scientists and astronomers of his time. His journey started at the age of five when he would find books about stars in his local library. After attending New York World’s Fair and becoming a fan of science-fiction, his interest in the stars, flying saucers, and astronomy grew substantually.

After graduating high school in 1951, Carl Sagan attended the University of Chicago at the age of 16 and obtained a Bachelor's of Science in Physics, and later a Masters in physics also from the University of Chicago. His PH.D. was completed degree in 1960 with his thesis Physical Studies of the Planets submitted to the Department of Astronomy and Astrophysics.

With his fascination for alien life growing, he worked as a fellow of astronomy at the University of California, Berkeley, where he helped a team develop an infrared radiometer for NASA’s Mariner 2 robotic probe. In 1968, he was named director of Cornell’s Laboratory for Planetary Studies, and three years later, he became a full professor.

Fringe Science

Sagan began speaking out on fringe issues. The feasibility of interstellar flight was one such issue that gained attention. He also coined the idea that aliens visited the Earth thousands of years ago and that creatures resembling “gas bags” live high in Jupiter's atmosphere. He also testified before Congress during this period about UFOs and proposed terraforming Venus into a habitable world.

Career

Sagan briefly served as a consultant on the Stanley Kubrick film 2001: A Space Odyssey and also launched the largely influential TV series Cosmos: A Personal Voyage. Sagan also wrote several books that helped launch him into stardom. Works such as :

  • The Cosmic Connection: An Extraterrestrial Perspective (1973)
  • Other Worlds (1975)
  • The Dragons of Eden: Speculations on the Evolution of Human Intelligence (1977; Pulitzer Prize winner)
  • Contact (1985)
  • The Cold and the Dark: The World After Nuclear War (1985)
  • Pale Blue Dot: A Vision of the Human Future in Space (1994)

Legacy

On December 20, 1996, at age 62, Carl Edward Sagan died of pneumonia. Eighteen years later, Cosmos was brought back to TV, this time with Neil DeGrasse Tyson taking on hosting duties and getting a whole new generation of viewers excited about what lies beyond the boundaries of Earth's atmosphere.

Sagan received many honors with the most prominant NASA’s Distinguished Public Service Medal, in 1977 and 1981, and the National Academy of Sciences’ Public Welfare Medal in 1994.
